Introduction to El-Adaima

The Ancient Egyptian site of el-Adaima is located along the west side of the Nile River. This particular site was inhabited from approximately 3700-2600 BC (). Adaima was discovered by a man named Jacques de Morgan in 1908. He was a French archaeologist who happened to be working in the area at the time of his grand discovery. Excavations of the site began shortly thereafter. After de Morgan, the site was left abandoned until it was rediscovered by Ferdinand Debono in 1973 (). Debono then began his own excavations into 30 different tombs. All of these gravesites were badly plundered by the time of his excavations, and nowadays, unfortunately, this section of the cemetery is completely destroyed.

Description of Site

The site of el-Adaima is divided into three distinct sections. One section of the site is known as the Western Cemetery. Unfortunately, there were very few intact graves in this particular area. These burials were badly plundered, presumably during the Predynastic Period.

Conclusion

Overall, the site of el-Adaima was an important archaeological find. Its uniqueness in being a site with a settlement and gravesite that was used simultaneously was important because it allowed for comparisons to be made. Archaeologists could compare information from the settlement to the cemetery and analyze components such as social status, wealth, and religious beliefs. It is a shame that so much of the site was damaged and plundered.
