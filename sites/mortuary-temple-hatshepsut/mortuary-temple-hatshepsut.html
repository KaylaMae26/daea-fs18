Hatshepsut’s mortuary temple was her principal achievement in architecture, as it was built according to her own plans. (Metropolitan Museum of Art), It was built directly out of the side of a limestone cliff, with three levels and many many statues.

The Mortuary Temple of Hatshepsut's location is significant for more than one reason. The closeness of the Temple of Mentuhotep II’s temple is not coincidence. Mentuhotep II was likely already believed to have brought upon the beginning of the second golden age, because of the placement of her temple next to his Hatshepsut may have been implying that she would also bring upon a golden age. (Metropolitan Museum of Art).

It is also very close to the Valley of the Kings, which is on the opposite side of the rock massif from Hatshepsut’s temple. The temple, also called Djeser-djeser, translated to Holiest of Holies in english, closely follows the same structure of the Old Kingdom pyramid complexes, which is as follows; Tomb, upper temple, causeway, valley temple (Cwiek, pg. 67). In fact, the rock structure separating the temple from the Valley of the Kings could be considered a sort of natural pyramid.

Destruction/Defacing

Though she was the main ruler of Egypt for about 20 years, Hatshepsut was quickly erased from history. Thutmose III, who was her co-ruler and nephew had ordered for any reference to Hatshepsut's rule to be destroyed. In the 1920s and 1930s the Metropolitan Museum of Art discovered two great pits with smashed statues from The Mortuary Temple of Hatshepsut, which was quickly determined to be the work of Thutmose III. (Metropolitan Museum of Art, 2005). Much of what the Temple originally looked like has been forever lost because the destruction ordered by Thutmose III. Thutmose III had originally been filling the gaps with offerings, specifically where he stood behind her (this was representative of Hatshepsut being the main ruler and Thutmose III being second to her), though after some time he instead decided to change her temple into one for his father and Hatshepsut’s late husband Thutmose II. Carvings of the female pharaoh were recarved to show Thutmose II. (Metropolitan Museum of Art). In addition, on the walls of the temple where Hatshepsut’s name would have originally been written has been scratched out while Thutmose III’s name remains intact directly next to the reference to the female pharaoh.
