Amarna

Late Eighteenth Dynasty

Liz Christensen

Introduction

Known today as its Arabic names, el-Amarna or Tell-el-Amarna, Amarna was the capital city during the reign of Pharaoh Akhenaten and Queen Nefertiti. Amarna was built sometime during the 4th or 5th year of Pharaoh Akhenaten’s reign (1353-1336 BCE) and remained the capital of Egypt for 15-20 years until Akhenaten’s death when it was later moved to Memphis and then to Thebes by his successor, Tutankhamun (Originally Tutankhaten).

During its habitation, Amarna was known then by its Egyptian name, Akhetaten, meaning “Horizon of Aten (or Aton)”. Aten was originally a minor deity with its name translated to light and represented with a sun disk hieroglyph (Ancient Egypt Gods: The Aten.” Pyramids of Ancient Egypt: Bent Pyramid of Sneferu, Dashur). Amarna was built to worship Aten and Akhenaten chose to build his new capital in this location because he thought the site had never been used to worship any other god.

Amarna is an exception to what archaeologists typically see of a site because Amarna was not substantially occupied before or after the time of the capital and it therefore does not have a lot of urban buildup as is typically seen with Ancient Egyptian sites. Therefore, Amarna offers a large array of urban landscape that has been continually excavated since the 19th century.
