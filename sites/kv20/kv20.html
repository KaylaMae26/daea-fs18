Excavations: Past and Present

Most of the excavations done in KV20 have been done before the twenty-first century. When Napoleon and the French forces arrived in Egypt, the Commission des Sciences et des Arts mapped and planned the tomb in 1799 (“General Site Information”); however, they did not go inside. In 1817, Giovanni Battista Belzoni (“General Site Information”), an Italian strongman, described the tomb (Hawass) as he mapped and planned it as well. In the 1824-1825 season, James Burton managed to clear KV20 to the second stairway (Hawass), or corridor D1, along with mapping and planning it (“General Site Information”). Carl Richard Lepsius mapped and planned the tomb as well during the 1844-1845 season (“General Site Information”). Conducting the excavation for Theodore M. Davis in 1903 to 1904 (“General Site Information”), Howard Carter fully excavated the tomb, though he did point out that it was a difficult tomb to enter, partially because of the location and partially because of how badly it smelled like bat droppings (Hawass). He discovered Hatshepsut’s sarcophagus (History.com), and was the one to determine that KV20 was a tomb for both Hatshepsut and Thutmose I (Hawass). Some recent excavations occurred in KV60, a tomb in front of KV20 (Hawass) during the mid-2000s, searching for Hatshepsut’s mummy; it was eventually found in 2007 (Hawass). Due to the location and the rough condition of some of the corridors and other areas of the tomb, it is hard to actually excavate in KV20, but, due to the diligence of historical excavations, much is known about the site.

In the burial chamber, a sarcophagus for Hatshepsut and a recurved sarcophagus that was originally Hatshepsut’s that became Thutmose I’s were found during Carter’s excavation (Hawass). The sarcophagus for Hatshepsut was made of Quartzite, faced south, and rested on plinth blocks (“Burial chamber J2”). It was decorated with the four sons of Horus, Anubis, Isis, and Nephthys on the exterior, Nut on the box interior and both sides of the lid, along with text (“Burial chamber J2”). The sarcophagus was 2.45 meters long, 0.87 meters wide, and 1 meter high (“Burial chamber J2”). Originally made for Hatshepsut, Thutmose I’s sarcophagus was enlarged so his coffin could fit inside, with a length of 2.23 meters, a width of 0.88 meters, and a height of 0.99 meters (“Burial chamber J2”). It was also made of Quartzite and faced south like Hatshepsut’s sarcophagus, but it was on its side instead of lying flat (“Burial chamber J2”). Unlike Hatshepsut’s elaborately decorated sarcophagus, Thutmose I’s was decorated with his right and left sides (“Burial chamber J2”). While no mummies were found in neither the tomb nor the sarcophagi, there were some other ritual burial items that were found in the tomb. Hatshepsut’s canopic jars were found (Hawass), which are used to store the internal organs. A box with her royal cartouches was also found, and it had a mummified spleen or liver inside of it (“General Site Information”), along with other organic material since anything body-related had to be buried properly (Hawass). A single tooth was also found in the box with Hatshepsut’s cartouches (Hawass). While this box was found near the tomb, not actually in it (Hawass), it still was an important find.

There are numerous other artifacts that were found in KV20. During his excavation, Carter discovered a shabti, which is a funerary statuette, and it looks like Hatshepsut (Hawass). He also discovered stone vessels which had her name on them, along with Thutmose I and Ahmose Nefertari’s names on them (Hawass). Faience pieces, or glazed ceramics, were found, along with head fragments and a small foot piece that might have come from a guardian statue (Hawass). Other objects that were discovered in KV20 included jewelry, sculptures, tomb equipment, and various vessels (“General Site Information”). Even though the tomb had been looted, some artifacts were still found in the tomb.


Results and Significance

Having all of these artifacts in the tomb is vitally important for learning more about the two rulers. After Hatshepsut’s rule, her son, Thutmose III, tried to get rid of any evidence of her reign (“Hatshepsut”). With KV20’s discovery and excavations, much can be gleaned about the reign of both Hatshepsut and her father. Not only that, because KV20 was one of the first tombs to be built in the Valley of the Kings, it provides plenty of valuable information when compared to the other tombs in the area. Due to its unique layout, it is visually distinct compared to the other tombs; though, as stated above, there are reasons as to why this layout may be different than later tombs.

The artifacts found in the tomb can provide fascinating information as well. The shabti that looks similar to Hatshepsut is one of the only ones, if not the only one, left of the famous pharaoh’s shabti (Hawass). This could be a result of either the tomb’s looting or Thutmose III’s erasing anything to do with Hatshepsut – or both. With modern day CT scans and DNA tests, the mummified spleen or liver and tooth that were found were tested to see if they matched any of the mummies found in KV60 (Hawass). While some scientists have their doubts, others, such as Zahi Hawass, the secretary general of the Supreme Council of Antiquities, believe that they have indeed found the actual mummy of Hatshepsut (Wilford). All of these artifacts, and KV20 itself, are helping archaeologists learn more about these two pharaohs.


Conclusion

One of the earliest tombs built in the Valley of the Kings, KV20 was originally built to only house Thutmose I before being adapted to hold both him and his daughter, Hatshepsut. It is located on the Western bank of the Nile, and has a distinct layout when it is compared to other tombs in the Valley. While conservation has been hard to do in the tomb, numerous different excavations, along with mapping and planning KV20, have occurred. Even with looting, there were still artifacts left to be found, ranging from sarcophagi to jewelry to some mummified remains. These artifacts, while few, are important because they help archaeologists learn more about Hatshepsut’s rule. Even though KV20 might not be as impressive as other tombs in the Valley of the Kings, it does provides an important look into Ancient Egyptian society.
