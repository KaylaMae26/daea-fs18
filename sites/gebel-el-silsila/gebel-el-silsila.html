Gebel el-Silsila

18th Dynasty

Paige Brewbaker

Introduction

The site of Gebel el-Silsila features the largest sandstone quarries in ancient Egypt. Gebel el-Silsila is famous for its many New Kingdom stelae, funerary shrines, and the rock-cut temple previously known as the Speos of Horemheb. A Swedish mission lead by Maria Nilsson and John ward has discovered 12 new tombs dating from the 18th Dynasty (Thutmosid period), including crypts cut into the rock, rock-cut tombs with one or two chambers, a tomb containing multiple animal burials, and several burials of children, some still intact.


Geography and Setting

The site of Gebel el-Silsila is located on both sides of the Nile in between Edfu and Kom Ombo in the Aswan region. Located at the point where the Nile river reaches its lowest point. The site is now divided into east (Gebel el Silsila) and west (Ramadi Gibli) with ancient quarries that run for 2.5 and 5 km along the Nile respectively. During the New Kingdom, Gebel el-Silsila significance was the topographic features including the extremely narrow river bed surrounded by sandstone hills and they believe that it is a place of religious importance as a place of worship. It was also a place of royal favor towardsindividuals. This resulted in a substantial number of monuments, from temples to graffiti, most of them cut into the rock and is still there.
